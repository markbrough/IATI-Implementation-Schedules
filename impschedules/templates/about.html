About

Publish What You Fund, the global campaign for aid transparency, developed this site to showcase and analyse the implementation schedules being released as part of the common standard for aid information.

Purpose

Since agreeing upon the common standard on aid information at the 4th High Level Forum on Aid Effectiveness at Busan in 2012, 39 donor organisations and governments have released implementation schedules outlining their plans for meeting the end of 2015 target date for publishing timely, comprehensive and forward-looking information on resources for development co-operation.

A great deal of new information on plans and systems is contained within the schedules which needs to be analysed in order to understand what donors are setting as their own targets and timeline for meeting the commitments made at Busan. Publish What You Fund carried out detailed comparative analysis of the implementation schedules to take stock of progress, highlight trends and provide feedback to donors on discrepancies in interpretation and approaches.

Methodology

We developed an online application in order to meaningfully assess and compare the various versions of donor implementation schedules currently available. This application has been designed to read both the IATI and common standard schedules and provides an easy front end onto otherwise daunting spreadsheets. It provides information on each field within the standard, when it will be published by different organisations, as well as a timeline of publication.

Scoring criteria

Donors were assessed on three key criteria: the intention to publish to the common standard / IATI; their publication approach; and their publication plans for each field at the agency and activity levels of the Standard.

Intention to publish
Stating intention to publish to the common standard / IATI with an explicit time frame indicated
Publication approach
Open license (public domain or attribution-only) and at least quarterly publication
Open license or quarterly publication, but not both
Neither open license nor quarterly publication
Fields
0-100%

A point was awarded for planning to publish by a specific date for each agency and activity-level field in the standard

Each field received a point if the publication status was marked ‘fully compliant’, ‘future publication’ with an explicit target date or ‘partially compliant’ with supplementary text suggesting plans for future publication by a specific target date.

Where there were inconsistencies between the status and the accompanying text descriptions, donors were not scored even if the publication status was marked ‘fully compliant.’

Fields were grouped under the sub-headings used in the common standard template

All groups were weighted equal, as were all fields within each group, to arrive at a final score in the range of 0 to 100.

Grouping

Based on the scores received, donors were placed in one of the following groups:

All the criteria of a group must be met to be placed in that group.

Some donors could not be grouped into any of the above groups, and were categorised as follows:

Notes

The following assumptions were made in order to ensure that the data was comparable across donors:

  • Not answering a question was understood as a “no” and received no score.
  • Using only the year in a date field was understood as the last day of the year.
  • The initial implementation date was used as the minimum publication date for all elements.
  • If the publication date for specific items was later than the initial implementation date, then that item-specific date was be used instead.

The following fields were not scored since they might not be relevant for all the donor agencies assessed at this point, depending on their particular business models:

  • Transactions (Reimbursement)
  • Transactions (Loan repayment / interest repayment)
  • Activity website
  • Sector (Agency specific)
  • Other Identifier

The scores and groups reflect only the level of ambition demonstrated by donors in their implementation schedules and not the quality or veracity of data that is being or will be published. In addition, because of the apparent variation in the way donors have understood and completed different fields within the schedule, some interpretation on our part has been inevitable to ensure consistency and comparability across templates. Donors may want to review their schedules and initiate a conversation with us on matters where they note discrepancies. For these same reasons, donors have not been ranked according to their total scores at this point in time.

Process

Analysing and processing the common standard schedules has required a detailed look at each donor's schedule, as well as interpreting them when donors have filled them out in different ways or understood the various options differently.

The original implementation schedules

The implementation schedules for the common standard were published in individual Excel files, containing three main sheets: general, agency, and activity-level information.

  • General

    Approach to publication: includes timeliness and frequency, license, initial publication dates, scope of publication.

  • Agency

    Agency-level publication: includes country budgets, organisation documents.

  • Activity

    Activity-level publication: includes information about when the organisation will (or won't) be compliant with each field in the standard.

Templates

While the common standard has a template for implementation schedules, several different templates exist, and donors further added to this complexity by modifying the templates, changing options, and adding and deleting rows – in all, we counted eleven different versions. In some cases, importing the schedules proved difficult because of this. In addition, some donors saved their templates in PDF format which made it impossible to automatically parse. In these cases, it was necessary to copy the same data into new spreadsheets. While the data was thoroughly checked, it is possible that some human error will have resulted. Some interpretation was necessary to ensure consistency and comparability across them (you can read more about the assumptions made above).

Importing

The schedules were automatically parsed and imported into this application. Publish What You Fund staff then checked the resulting data to ensure that it had been parsed correctly and that it made sense.

  • Select a file

    The user can select a schedule from any publicly-accessible URL. This could be the OECD/DAC common standard website, the IATI website, or the donor's own website.

    Where the original schedules could not be automatically parsed (e.g. because they were in PDF), a new file was created and uploaded to the Publish What You Fund website.

  • Successfully parsed

    The schedule was successfully parsed and is presented to the user to check and correct.

  • Check fields

    Make sure that all information has been parsed correctly, ensure that compliance status is consistent with notes, and score for partially compliant fields if the publisher had been overly conservative.
